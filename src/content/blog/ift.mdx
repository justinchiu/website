A [startup](https://x.com/opt_intellect/status/2036485190646735291?s=20) recently came out that reminded me of an old [report](https://github.com/justinchiu/ift) I wrote on differentiating through optimization problems with the [implicit function theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_function_theorem) (IFT).
If I remember correctly, I was playing with ways to differentiate through massively sparse softmax problems.
Unfortunately, I think I gave up after a week. Don't be me.

Regardless, the IFT is super cool! It lets you differentiate through implicitly defined functions.
For example, you want to optimize the parameters of an optimization problem.
The issue is that the solver for this problem uses gradient descent and therefore takes many steps.
Reverse-mode autodiff would require storing the entire execution trace to compute gradients, which requires memory linear in the number of iterations.
The IFT lets you throw all of that away and compute gradients using only the solution and the optimality conditions, which implicitly defines a function.

The IFT shows up in a number of places.
[OptNet](https://arxiv.org/abs/1703.00443) used it to backpropagate through quadratic program solvers, letting neural networks learn constraints (e.g. Sudoku from input-output pairs).
[Influence functions](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.05364) used it for training data attribution (I guess mainly [Anthropic](https://www.anthropic.com/research/influence-functions)).
Meta-learning methods like [MAML](https://arxiv.org/abs/1703.03400) differentiate through inner-loop optimization, and [iMAML](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.04630) used the IFT to do so without unrolling.
Hyperparameter optimization and variational inference also fit naturally into this framework.
I'm also old and haven't kept up with this area, so there could be even more.

In this post, I'll walk through the mechanics of applying the IFT to softmax, expressed as an optimization problem.
Warning: This post goes a bit deeper into calculations than my previous posts as the IFT is pretty mechanical. Good thing we have AI.

## The Implicit Function Theorem
As a starting example, consider the unit circle
governed by the relation
$$
F(\theta, x) = \theta^2 + x^2 - 1 = 0.
$$
Computing the derivative $\frac{dx}{d\theta}$ is not straightforward, as $F$ fails the vertical line test and we cannot write $x$ as a function of $\theta$ globally.
However, we can find local parameterizations: on the upper semicircle ($x > 0$), $x = f_1(\theta) = \sqrt{1 - \theta^2}$, and on the lower semicircle ($x < 0$), $x = f_2(\theta) = -\sqrt{1 - \theta^2}$.
These let us compute $\frac{dx}{d\theta}$ at particular solution points, though not everywhere (e.g. not at $x = 0$).

so $\frac{dx}{d\theta}$ exists there.
At $p_2 = (1, 0)$, the circle is vertical, so no local parameterization exists and the derivative is undefined.
</figcaption>
</figure>

The IFT generalizes this. Instead of finding a local parameterization, it guarantees one exists and tells you its derivative. The parameterization is left *implicit*, hence the name.

Formally, given a system of equations $F(\theta, x) = \mathbf{0}_m$
and a solution point $(\theta, x) \in \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^m$,
the IFT says there exists a local solution mapping $x^*(\theta)$ if:

1. We have a solution $F(\theta, x) = 0$.
2. Continuous first derivatives $F \in \mathcal{C}^1$.
3. The Jacobian $\frac{dF(\theta, x)}{dx}$ is nonsingular at the solution point.

When these hold, the derivative of the solution mapping is
$$
\frac{d x^*(\theta)}{d\theta}
=
-\left[\frac{dF(\theta, x)}{dx}\right]^{-1}
\frac{dF(\theta, x)}{d\theta}
\in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}.
$$
The key point: as long as the conditions hold at a solution point, we can compute this derivative regardless of how we arrived at that solution.

## The Softmax Optimization Problem
Let's apply this to something concrete: softmax.
Softmax has a known Jacobian, so we can verify the IFT gives the right answer.
The exercise is to express softmax as an optimization problem, then derive its Jacobian using the IFT.

Softmax is defined as follows.
Given $n$ items with independent utilities, where $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^n$,
softmax yields a distribution over items:
$$
z_i = \mathrm{softmax}(\theta)_i = \frac{\exp(\theta_i)}{\sum_j \exp(\theta_j)},
$$
with $z \in [0,1]^n$.

Softmax is also the solution of the following constrained optimization problem:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\textrm{maximize } \quad & z^\top\theta + H(z)\\
\textrm{subject to } \quad & z^\top \mathbf{1} = 1\\
& z_i \geq 0, \forall i,
\end{aligned}
$$
where $H(z) = -\sum_i z_i \log z_i$ is the entropy.

Our goal is to compute the Jacobian of softmax
$$
\frac{dz}{d\theta} = \frac{d\,\mathrm{softmax}(\theta)}{d\theta}
$$
using the IFT and the optimization problem above.

## Applying the IFT
Applying the IFT consists of four steps:

1. Find a solution to the optimization problem.
2. Write down a system of equations derived from the optimality conditions.
3. Check that the conditions of the IFT hold.
4. Compute the derivative of the implicit solution mapping with respect to the parameters.

We assume the first step has been done for us, and we have a solution $z$ to the softmax problem.

### Step 2: The KKT conditions determine the system of equations
In order to apply the IFT,
we need a system of equations for which our outputs of interest are solution points.
For solutions of optimization problems, the [Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karush-Kuhn-Tucker_conditions) conditions
are a natural choice for defining such a system of equations.
Given an optimization problem, the KKT conditions
determine a system of equations that the solution must satisfy
based on the optimality criteria.
They are stationarity (the gradient should be $0$ at a local optimum)
and feasibility (the constraints of the problem should not be violated).

We will use the KKT conditions of the softmax problem to determine the vector-valued function
$F : \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^{n+1} \to \mathbb{R}^{n+1}$
in the IFT.
The optimization problem has both equality and inequality constraints,
but for finite $\theta$ the softmax solution is always strictly positive:
$$
z_i = \frac{\exp(\theta_i)}{\sum_j \exp(\theta_j)} > 0.
$$
That means the inequality constraints are inactive at the solution points we care about,
so it is cleaner to work with the reduced KKT system containing only the equality constraint.
We therefore introduce only the equality multiplier $u \in \mathbb{R}$
and write out the Lagrangian:
$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta, z, u)
=
z^\top\theta + H(z) + u(z^\top \mathbf{1}_n - 1).
$$
We therefore have the solution point $(\theta, z, u)$, with parameters $\theta$
and solution $x = (z, u)$.
We then have the following necessary conditions for a solution $(z, u)$,
i.e. the KKT conditions:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial}{\partial z} \mathcal{L}(\theta, z, u) &= \mathbf{0}_n && \textrm{(stationarity)}\\
z^\top \mathbf{1}_n - 1 &= 0 && \textrm{(primal feasibility, equality)}\\
z &\succeq \mathbf{0}_n && \textrm{(primal feasibility, inequality)}.
\end{aligned}
$$
As we only need a system of equations with $n+1$ equations to
determine the $n+1$ solution variables $x = (z, u)$,
we use the first two conditions: stationarity and primal feasibility (equality).

In full, the system of equations $F(\theta, z, u) = 0$ we choose
for the softmax problem is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\theta - \log(z) - \mathbf{1}_n + u\mathbf{1}_n &= \mathbf{0}_n\\
z^\top \mathbf{1}_n - 1 &= 0.
\end{aligned}
$$
Note that the first equation is vector-valued.

This completes the second step, where we chose a subset of the KKT conditions
in order to produce a nonlinear system of equations.
We can now proceed to check the conditions of the IFT,
which will determine whether $F$ is locally well-behaved
at a particular solution point $(\theta, z, u)$.

### Step 3: Check that the IFT conditions hold at the solution point
Recall that the IFT requires the following three conditions,
which must be checked on a case-by-case basis for particular
solution points.
If these conditions do not hold,
the IFT does not apply at this particular combination of $F$ and solution point.
The derivative $\frac{dx}{d\theta}$ may still be computed via other means
if it exists.

- $F(\theta, z, u) = 0$,
- $F$ has at least continuous first derivatives,
- $\det \frac{dF(\theta, z, u)}{d(z, u)} \ne 0$,
  or equivalently $\frac{dF(\theta, z, u)}{d(z, u)}$
  is full rank.

Returning to the softmax problem,
the first condition holds as we have a solution to the optimization problem
and $F$ was chosen using the KKT conditions.
Recall that softmax also has a closed form expression.
The second condition also holds, as $F$ has continuous first derivatives.
All that remains is to check the third condition,
that the Jacobian matrix
$$
\frac{dF(\theta, z, u)}{d(z, u)}
$$
(evaluated at the solution point) is non-singular.

The Jacobian matrix
$$
\frac{dF(\theta, z, u)}{d(z, u)} \in \mathbb{R}^{n+1 \times n+1}
$$
is given by
$$
\frac{dF(\theta, z, u)}{d(z, u)}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
    -\mathrm{diag}(z)^{-1} & \mathbf{1}_n \\
    \mathbf{1}_n^\top & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$
The upper-left block is $-\mathrm{diag}(z)^{-1}$ (from the Hessian of $H$); its entries blow up if
any component $z_i = 0$.
This term was obtained by differentiating the entropy
term of the Lagrangian,
$$
H(z) = -\sum_i z_i \log z_i.
$$
While we could use the convention $0 \log 0 = 0$,
this does not fix the divide-by-zero issue with the second derivative, which we see here.
To avoid this, we consider only strictly positive $z \succ 0$ for the IFT.
This restriction is natural here, as softmax already satisfies $z_i > 0$ for finite $\theta$.
We saw a similar issue in the unit circle example, where the derivative
$\frac{dx}{d\theta}$ was undefined when $x=0$.
Once $z \succ 0$, the block $-\mathrm{diag}(z)^{-1}$ is invertible.
The Schur complement of the upper-left block is
$$
0 - \mathbf{1}_n^\top\left(-\mathrm{diag}(z)\right)\mathbf{1}_n
=
z^\top \mathbf{1}_n
=
1,
$$
where we used primal feasibility.
Therefore the Jacobian of $F$ is nonsingular.
This shows that the conditions of the IFT hold for the solution points that are
feasible, optimal, and have strictly positive $z$.
Recall that the IFT applies at particular solution points,
meaning we can pick and choose which points to analyze.

### Step 4: Compute $\frac{dx}{d\theta}$
Now that we have a set of solution points where conditions of the IFT hold,
we can use the IFT to compute $\frac{dz}{d\theta}$.
Recall that we have the solution $x = (z, u)$; we will switch to $x$ for brevity.
The second part of the IFT tells us that we can compute the Jacobian of the
solution mapping
$$
\frac{dx}{d\theta}
=
\frac{dx^*(\theta)}{d\theta}
=
-\left[\frac{dF(\theta, x)}{dx}\right]^{-1}\frac{dF(\theta, x)}{d\theta},
$$
then pick out the relevant components.

The second term,
$$
\frac{dF(\theta, x)}{d\theta},
$$
is simple.
Since $\theta$ only appears in the first vector-valued function of $F$,
we have
$$
\frac{dF(\theta, x)}{d\theta}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
    I_{n \times n}\\
    \mathbf{0}_{1 \times n}
\end{bmatrix}.
$$
Rather than write down the full inverse explicitly,
it is simpler to solve the linear system induced by the IFT identity.
Differentiating the equations in $F(\theta, z, u)=0$ gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
d\theta - \mathrm{diag}(z)^{-1}dz + \mathbf{1}_n\,du &= \mathbf{0}_n\\
\mathbf{1}_n^\top dz &= 0.
\end{aligned}
$$
Solving the first equation for $dz$ yields
$$
dz = \mathrm{diag}(z)\left(d\theta + \mathbf{1}_n\,du\right).
$$
Substituting this into the equality constraint gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
0
&=
\mathbf{1}_n^\top dz\\
&=
\mathbf{1}_n^\top \mathrm{diag}(z)\left(d\theta + \mathbf{1}_n\,du\right)\\
&=
z^\top d\theta + \left(z^\top \mathbf{1}_n\right)du\\
&=
z^\top d\theta + du,
\end{aligned}
$$
where we again used feasibility: $z^\top \mathbf{1}_n = 1$.
Therefore
$$
du = -z^\top d\theta.
$$
Plugging this back in,
$$
\begin{aligned}
dz
&=
\mathrm{diag}(z)\left(d\theta - \mathbf{1}_n z^\top d\theta\right)\\
&=
\left(\mathrm{diag}(z) - zz^\top\right)d\theta.
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore the Jacobian of the solution mapping is
$$
\frac{d z}{d\theta} = \mathrm{diag}(z) - zz^\top,
$$
which agrees with directly differentiating softmax.
For this toy example, the special structure of the KKT system lets us solve the
linear system in closed form.
In more general problems, solving the IFT system typically requires
$O(n^3)$ time and $O(n^2)$ space in the dense case.

This concludes the exercise of using the IFT to differentiate through the softmax problem.
While softmax itself has a closed form expression for both its output and derivative,
we showed that the IFT can also be used to compute its derivative in a solver-agnostic manner.
We started by expressing softmax as an optimization problem,
then, assuming we had a solution to the softmax problem regardless of whether it was obtained
in closed form or through an iterative method,
reverse engineered a system of equations using the KKT conditions.
We then restricted our attention to the set of solution points where we could successfully
apply the IFT,
then applied the derivative identity above to compute the derivative at a solution point.

The same methodology can be applied to more general optimization problems,
such as quadratic programs (as in OptNet) or other convex programs.
In those problems closed form solutions may not exist, and iterative solvers must be used
where applying reverse-mode AD would take $\Omega(Kn)$ space with $K$ steps,
while the IFT would naively take $O(n^2)$ space.

### Limitations
In order to compute the derivative $\frac{dx}{d\theta}$, we had to invert the Jacobian of $F$
(the system of equations derived from the KKT conditions),
i.e. compute
$$
\left[\frac{dF(\theta, x)}{dx}\right]^{-1}.
$$
However, the first part of $F$, the stationarity condition
$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\mathcal{L} = 0,
$$
already involved the Jacobian of the Lagrangian $\mathcal{L}$.
In general, this means that in order to apply the IFT to solutions of optimization problems,
we must compute the inverse Hessian of the Lagrangian.
From the IFT, we can either compute the full inverse Hessian or only the
inverse-Hessian-vector product, which can sometimes be cheaply approximated.
The Hessian is a matrix of size $O(n^2)$,
and inverting this would take $O(n^3)$ computation.
The number of solution variables scales with the number of primal variables,
but also the number of constraints.
This potentially makes applying the IFT to optimization problems with exponentially
many constraints difficult.
Thankfully, there are relatively cheap ways of approximating this computation,
such as with approximate inverse-Hessian-vector-product techniques.
Lorraine et al. in particular show that the derivative can be approximated
in $O(n)$ space using a truncated Neumann series approximation for the inverse Hessian.

Additionally, comparing the speed of computing derivatives via AD on a large trace versus the IFT
must be done on a case-by-case basis.
For softmax, let us pretend we solved the softmax problem with an iterative method in $K$ steps.
Computing the derivative from the execution trace would take $O(Kn)$ time using reverse-mode AD,
while the naive IFT would take $O(n^3)$ time, giving an asymptotic speedup only when $K > n^2$.
In practice, the constant factors in the runtime will play a large factor in which approach
is better, as well as any approximations made to make either approach more efficient.
For unrolled AD, we could not fully solve the optimization problem and use the truncated trace.
For the IFT, vector-inverse-Hessian-vector-product approximations other than the Neumann series
include conjugate gradient, identity approximations, and other factorizations such as K-FAC.

### Extensions
The methods we covered can be extended to variations of argmax problems
other than the softmax problem.
The argmax problem is given by maximize $z^\top \theta$, subject to $z^\top\mathbf{1}_n = 1$
and $z \succeq 0$, otherwise known as the support function of the constraint set.
The softmax problem altered the argmax problem by introducing entropy regularization.
Rather than regularizing with entropy, one could instead alter the objective to find
the Euclidean projection of the parameters onto the probability simplex,
resulting in SparseMax.
While the output of softmax variants often have a closed form expression,
the IFT provides another way of deriving their Jacobians
and could potentially pave the way for differentiating through argmax problems
that do not have closed-form expressions,
such as projections onto the $(n,k)$-simplex,
which can be interpreted as selecting $k$ elements from $n$.

More generally, the IFT can be applied to cases where, unlike softmax,
we do not have an explicit functional form and outputs are governed only by a system of equations.
This includes more general optimization problems, such as quadratic programs
or other convex optimization problems.

## Discussion
In this post, we walked through the application of the implicit function theorem
to optimizing the parameters of an optimization problem, as a function of a solution.
The process was quite involved, involving a large number of manual calculations.
Ironically, this contrasts with the philosophy of **automatic** differentiation,
which seeks to minimize user error by minimizing the number of manual calculations.
The degree of manual calculation in applying the IFT is unsurprising,
as it generalizes forward- and reverse-mode automatic differentiation.
Griewank and Walther show that we can derive forward- and reverse-mode automatic differentiation
from the IFT, through Schur complements as we showed for the particular case of softmax.

Work by Blondel et al. seeks to address this by applying automatic differentiation to the
system of equations central to the application of the IFT to bilevel optimization.
We are excited to see where these methods find applications.
